Educational Product Design

Facing Freedom Web Application

In 2010 the Chicago History Museum opened Facing Freedom—A semi-permanent American History exhibition, which highlights some of the ways Americans have struggled over the true meaning of freedom. The exhibition is targeted at middle school & high-school students, so the Museum's education department invited me to help them create a complementary classroom experience. Together we created the Facing Freedom web application.

My Role

I was the user experience lead on the team, which inlcuded: two Museum educators, a visual designer, and a developer. My core responsibilities on the team were ideation, interaction design, visual design direction, usability testing, and liason between design and development.

Ideation

The physical exhibition presents four ways Americans have defined freedom: Workers Rights, Armed Conflict, Race & Citizenship, and Public Protest. Through these four themes, you can explore eight powerful American stories.

We knew that we would provide the exhibition stories as the context of the application, but needed the classroom teacher perspective to shape the added value of the application. To get that perspective we invited 10 Chicago teachers to form an advisory board. The teachers represented a wide sample of Chicago schools: public, private, affluent, and poor. Their job was to guide us in creating a classroom experience that they would use, and would resonate with their students.

After a couple of workshops with the teacher advisory board, we landed on three key experience requirements:

  1. Show & Tell—Let them explore the artifacts up close.
  2. Help Them Think—Connect what they've learned about the past to their life.
  3. Make It Social—Create a dialogue between students, teachers, and history.

We implemented three features to meet the above requirements: An artifact gallery, Discuss History Today, and My freedom collection.

Discuss History Today

The clouds are actions taken by the user or system, and the rectangles are views.

  • Overview

    Discuss History Today is a feature that meets the help them think, and make it social experience requirements. The student reads the story, the story introduces a historian, the historian prompts the student with a question that encourages the student to connect what they learned in the story to a current event, and this initiates a dialogue between the students, the historian, and history.

  • Discussion

    The historian initiates the discussion and serves as a moderator by providing feedback to student replies. Discussions are open and closed based on a pre-determined interval that best suits the teacher and historian schedule, and prior to closing they notify both the teachers and historians via e-mail. This notification alerts the historian to encourage them to provide feedback, and provide a closing remark. For teachers the alert serves to encourage their class to review the historian's feedback, and reflect on the dialogue.

My Freedom Collection

The My Freedom Collection feature is a freeform interpretation exercise that encourages students to makes a personal connection with history. The student browses a gallery containing all of the Facing Freedom artifacts, they select the object(s) that resonate with them, and express their perspective through writing.

The student is given the ability to share their interpretation via e-mail. The sharing feature enables teachers to use the My Freedom Collection feature as an assignment that encourages a thoughtful dialogue with their class.

Contextual Inquiry & Usability Testing

While the functional application prototype was being developed I performed expert reviews to catch the obvious ux issues and prepare it for user testing. I worked closely with the educators to create a contextual inquiry plan, contextual usability test, facilitator script, and assign test roles.

When the prototype was ready, five teachers from our advisory board invited us into their classrooms to test the prototype with their students.

Contextual Inquiry

The purpose of the contextual inquiry was to increase our understanding and ability to design classroom experiences for teachers and student’s. While performing the usability test I documented equipment available and used, the classroom setting, time allowed in the setting, Use of paper and other non-technology materials and tools, input preferences (mouse, keyboard, touch, etc), and collaboration and interactions among students and with their teacher.
