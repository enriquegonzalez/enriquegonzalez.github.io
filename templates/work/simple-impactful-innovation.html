The Chicago History Museum has over two million photographs in its collection. Only a small sample of the collection is digitized, and before I started this project it was not publicly accessible. You had to physically visit the Museum’s research center in order to navigate your way through a small portion the images. There were no resources for an online Digital Asset Management system; no money to buy one, and no one to create it.

It is the Museum’s mission to share Chicago’s story. The images resonated with me; I considered them to be among the Museum’s most compelling storytelling materials, so I made it a personal goal to help the Museum share them with the world.

My Role

As the Multimedia Designer for the Museum I was constantly discovering its great images. I wanted to share the images, delight people with them, and hopefully use them to create a new wave of history enthusiasts; I wanted to create the National Geographic of urban history.

My idea involved using social media to present the images to the public. Using social media provided us with a low-cost technical solution, it allowed us to ship fast, and enabled us to leverage our existing communities to promote this new initiative.

To bring the idea to life I partnered with the photography collection stakeholders; developed the strategy, executed on it, and measured its impact.

Strategy & Execution

Get Buy-in

I pitched the idea to my supervisor and received her blessings. My next step was selling the idea to the photo collection stakeholders: the director of collections, the rights and reproductions team, and photolab staff.

The director of collections was hesitant because one of the Museum’s key businesses is the licensing and reproduction of the photographs, so preserving the image rights and license was a must.

We met with the Museum’s legal advisor and reviewed the Terms of Service for five major social networks. In the end I received legal sign-off on Tumblr, and approval from the director of collections. That’s when I created the experiment: Chicago History in Pictures—a Tumblr photoblog.

We would know if this experiment worked if our Tumblr followers grew at a steady rate, and if followers consistently liked or shared our images.

Share Ownership & Establish Workflow

Creating the Tumblr theme and photoblog was only the beginning. In order for this system to work I needed to establish a manageable workflow that guaranteed quality content for our audience without compromising the Museum’s license, or consuming too many resources. Results

  1. Tumblr Fame— People loved the photos and a few went viral. Three times during the photoblog’s first year Tumblr contacted me to tell me how much they loved the blog and to ask permission to spotlight it.

  2. Community Development—Our Tumblr followers grew from zero to over 300,000 in less than a year.

  3. Press Coverage—The Huffington Post began featuring our photos on a regular basis.

  4. Museum Photo Store—Because of the pilot’s success the Museum has evolved it into an online photo store, where you can now purchase prints and even have them printed on a t-shirt or iPhone case.
